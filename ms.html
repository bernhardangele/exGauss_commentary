Over the past two decades, psychology have seen a substantial increase in the application of Bayesian data analysis methods as a supplement to widely used freqeuentist methods (Pfadt et al., 2025). This shift has been driven by a number of factors including: increased computational resources, growing dissatisfaction with null-hypothesis significance testing (NHST) that arose from the ashes of the replication crisis, and the emergence of accessible software and packages in a variety of different language that allows researchers to fit bayesian models with relatively little technical overhead. Software like JASP and JAMOVI, and packages in R (e.g., {brms}, {easyststats}, etc) and several Python-based packages {Pymc} have made Bayesian workflows increasingly commonplace.

One domain in which Bayesian modeling has been especially helpful is reaction-time (RT) research in cognitive science, where RTs are routinely used as measures of processing speed, decision difficulty, and cognitive efficiency. Traditionally, RT data have often been analyzed using summary statistics such as the mean or median. However, RT distributions are typically positively skewed, bounded at zero, and characterized by substantial variability both within and between individuals (wilcox?) . Moreover, experimental manipulations may affect not only the central tendency of the distribution but also its variability or upper tail. Consequently, analyses based solely on the mean or median can obscure theoretically meaningful distributional effects, motivating calls to move beyond mean-based analyses of RT data [@balota2011].

One distributional model that has become especially popular for analyzing RT data is the ex-Gaussian model. The ex-Gaussian distribution is formed by the convolution of a Gaussian distribution and an exponential distribution and is characterized by three parameters: μ, σ, and τ. The parameter μ represents the mean of the Gaussian component, whereas σ represents its standard deviation. The parameter τ represents both the mean and standard deviation of the exponential component and primarily governs the length of the distribution’s positively skewed upper tail. The overall mean of the ex-Gaussian distribution is μ + τ, and its variance is σ² + τ². While these parameters have been used to infer cognitive cognitive processes such as automaticaity and congnitive control/inhibiton, these interpretations are not inherent to the parameters and should be made cautiously (Matzke & Wagenmakers, 2009).

The ex-Gaussian model can be fit within a Bayesian framework relatively easily using the brms package in R which provides a simple interface to the STAN langauge. The package supports a wide range of response distributions, including the ex-Gaussian family, and allows researchers to incorporate complex model structures at both the participant and item levels as well as across different parameters. This accessibility has contributed to the growing use of Bayesian ex-Gaussian models in reaction-time research. However, an important complication arises from the default parameterization used by brms. Although the model is straightforward to estimate, its default specification does not necessarily provide direct access to the parameter-specific comparisons researchers often wish to make. In particular, fitting your model with the default family provided by brms does not map onto the conventional ex-Gaussian parameters, (\mu), (\sigma), and (\tau). Consequently, researchers may believe that they are testing differences in the Gaussian component of the RT distribution when the fitted coefficient instead pertains to the overall mean or another transformed quantity. Appropriate distributional formulas and careful attention to the package’s parameterization are therefore required to obtain the intended inferences.

The central problem is that the parameter labeled (\mu) in the default brms ex-Gaussian model does not correspond to the mean of the Gaussian component in the conventional parameterization. Instead, the quantity modeled as (\mu) in brms represents the mean of the full ex-Gaussian distribution. Under the conventional parameterization, this quantity is equal to (\mu_{\mathrm{Gaussian}} + \tau). Consequently, regression effects estimated for the default (\mu) parameter reflect changes in a composite quantity that combines the Gaussian location and exponential tail components. Such effects therefore cannot be interpreted directly as changes in the Gaussian component or in typical response speed. To recover the conventional Gaussian mean, it is necessary to derive it from the model parameters as (\mu_{\mathrm{Gaussian}} = \mu_{\mathrm{brms}} - \tau) or to specify an alternative parameterization that models the conventional ex-Gaussian parameters directly.

In this commentary, I demonstrate what the default parameterization of the ex-Gaussian model in brms actually provides and then show how to specify the model so that its parameters correspond to the conventional ex-Gaussian parameters used in reaction-time research. The goal is to improve the accuracy and interpretability of inferences drawn from these models. This issue is particularly important because the ex-Gaussian distribution is widely used to distinguish changes in the central portion of the RT distribution from changes in its posietively skewed tail.

Example

To demonstrate the issue with fitting the default ex-Gaussian model in brms, I simulate data using a comparison adapted from one of my own studies [@geller2025]. This allows the methodological issue to be illustrated without singling out any particular researchers or journals, although published examples of this interpretive problem do exist (see )

In that study, we investigated how perceptual disfluency, operationalized by varying the amount of visual blur applied to words, influenced reaction times during encoding in a lexical decision task (i.e., is this letter string a word?). We were specifically interested in whether low and high levels of blur affected the Gaussian location parameter, (\mu), and the exponential tail parameter, (\tau), and whether these effects were subsequently related to recognition memory. For the present demonstration, I focus on the comparison between clearly presented words and words presented with a low level of blur. Here we simulate data from an ex-gaussian distribution using the {brms.exgaussian} package functuon rexgauss(). The conditions are specified to differ in the classical Gaussian location parameter, (\mu), but not in the exponential parameter, (\(\tau\)). Specifically, (\(\mu\)) is set to 500 ms for clear words and 550 ms for low-blur words. Default brms Parameterization

We first fit the default brms ex-Gaussian model. In this parameterization, the parameter labeled mu represents the expected value of the full ex-Gaussian distribution rather than the location of its Gaussian component.

The exponential component is labeled beta. On the response scale, beta corresponds to the classical ex-Gaussian parameter \(\tau\):

\[ \beta_{\texttt{brms}} = \tau_{\text{classical}}. \]

Prior Specification

The priors were selected to reflect the known scale of the simulated data while remaining sufficiently broad to allow the model to recover deviations from the generating values.

The intercept prior for the primary mu submodel is

\[ \mu_{\text{Intercept}} \sim \mathcal{N}(600,100). \]

We center this prior at 600 ms because, in the default brms parameterization, mu represents the expected RT. The simulated expected RT for the reference group is

\[ 500 + 100 = 600 \text{ ms}. \]

The standard deviation of 100 ms makes the prior informative about the plausible scale of RTs without tightly constraining the intercept to equal exactly 600 ms.

The prior on the group coefficient is

\[ b_{\text{Group}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,50). \]

This prior is centered at zero because, before observing the data, positive and negative condition effects are both plausible. A standard deviation of 50 ms allows substantial differences between conditions while placing less prior mass on implausibly large effects.

The beta parameter is positive and uses a log link. Its intercept is therefore modeled on the log scale:

\[ \log(\beta_{\text{Clear}}) \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\log(100),0.5\right). \]

The prior is centered at \(\log(100)\) because the simulated value of the exponential mean for the reference group is 100 ms. After applying the inverse-link transformation, the prior is centered approximately around

\[ \beta_{\text{Clear}} = 100 \text{ ms}. \]

The standard deviation of 0.5 is on the log scale. It therefore represents multiplicative rather than additive uncertainty.

The prior on the group effect for beta is

\[ b_{\beta,\text{Group}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,0.5). \]

Because this coefficient is also on the log scale, a value of zero represents no group difference:

\[ \exp(0)=1. \]

Prior Specification

For the classical model, the prior on the primary intercept is centered at 500 ms rather than 600 ms because mu now represents the Gaussian-location parameter:

The prior for the Gaussian-location intercept is

\[ \mu_{\text{Intercept}} \sim \mathcal{N}(500,100), \]

because the simulated Gaussian location for the Clear reference group is 500 ms.

The group coefficient again receives the prior

\[ b_{\text{Group}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,50). \]

The true simulated effect is 50 ms, so this prior allows the model to recover the expected difference while remaining centered at no effect.

The tau parameter uses a log link. Its intercept prior is therefore

\[ \log(\tau_{\text{Clear}}) \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\log(100),0.5\right). \]

This places the prior center at approximately 100 ms on the response scale.

The group effect on tau is given the prior

\[ b_{\tau,\text{Group}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,0.5). \]

The simulated decrease from 100 ms to 50 ms corresponds to

\[ \log\left(\frac{50}{100}\right) \approx -0.693, \]

which is plausible under this prior.

This prior is centered at the simulated Gaussian standard deviation of 60 ms. As with the priors for beta and tau, the prior is specified on the log scale because the parameter must remain positive.

This example illustrates the central difference between the two parameterizations. The default brms ex-Gaussian model directly estimates the expected response and labels that parameter mu. The exponential component is labeled beta, although it corresponds to the classical \(\tau\) parameter. In contrast, the {brms.exgaussian} model uses mu for the Gaussian-location parameter and tau for the exponential mean, consistent with the classical parameterization commonly used in reaction-time research.

Conclusion

With this commentary, I hope to encourage researchers to use the appropriate ex-Gaussian parameterization when modeling reaction-time data with the {brms} package. The ex-Gaussian distribution provides a powerful framework for moving beyond analyses of the mean by separating differences in the central portion of the reaction-time distribution from differences in its right tail.

However, this flexibility also requires careful attention to model parameterization. In particular, researchers should not assume that parameters with familiar labels necessarily have the same interpretation across software implementations. Researchers should therefore examine the statistical definition and underlying implementation of a model before fitting it and interpreting its parameters. This is especially important when using flexible modeling software that may adopt parameterizations that differ from those commonly used in a particular research literature. I hope this commentary serves both as a practical guide to fitting the classical ex-Gaussian model in {brms} and as a broader reminder that convenient software defaults should not replace careful consideration of what a model actually estimates.
