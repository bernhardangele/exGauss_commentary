Abstract

The ex-Gaussian distribution is a popular mathematical model for analyzing reaction-time (RT) data because it separates the central portion of the distribution, captured by the Gaussian parameters \mu and \sigma, from the positively skewed tail, captured by the exponential parameter \tau.Researchers often use these parameters to make inferences about underlying cognitive processes. As Bayesian methods have become increasingly accessible, complex ex-Gaussian models can now be fit with relative ease. In R, the {brms} package makes this especially straightforward. However, it is important to recognize that the default parameterization offered by {brms} does not map onto the classical parameterization familiar to many researchers in the cognitive sciences. As a result, some published studies may have drawn incorrect inferences from effects estimated on the default \mu parameter. In the default {brms} model, \mu indexes the mean of the full ex-Gaussian distribution rather than the location of the Gaussian component alone. This distinction matters because changes in the Gaussian location and changes in the exponential tail can offset one another at the level of the overall mean. Using simulated data in which blurred and clear words have Gaussian locations and exponential tail shifts that move in opposite directions—holding the overall distributional mean constant—I show that the default parameterization returns a null effect on \mu, whereas the classical parameterization recovers the true shift in the Gaussian component. I then demonstrate how to fit the classical parameterization directly in {brms}. My hope is that this commentary will help researchers and reviewers better understand the models they are fitting, ensure that model parameters match their theoretical questions, more accurately evaluate ex-Gaussian models reported in the literature.

Keywords: ex-Gaussian, reaction time, Bayesian estimation, brms, parameterization


This Is Not the ex-Gaussian Model You Are Looking For: On the Default Parameterization of Bayesian ex-Gaussian Models in brms

Over the past two decades, psychology has seen a substantial increase in the application of Bayesian data analysis methods as a supplement to widely used frequentist methods (Pfadt et al., 2025). This shift has been driven by the increasing availability of computational tools that have made fitting Bayesian models both practical and efficient. Programming languages like R (R Core Team, 2024), Python (Van Rossum & Drake, 2009), and Julia (Bezanson et al., 2017) provide libraries and packages that allow researchers to fit Bayesian models with little technical overhead. In addition, growing dissatisfaction with null-hypothesis significance testing (NHST) and its associated pitfalls (e.g., p-value misinterpretation and dichotomous thinking), together with the replication crisis/credibility revolution (Vazire, 2018), has pushed many researchers toward statistical alternatives.

One domain that has seen an increase in the adoption of Bayesian methods is the analysis of response time (RT) data in cognitive sciences where RTs are routinely used as measures of processing speed, decision difficulty, and cognitive efficiency. Traditionally, RT data have often been analyzed using summary statistics such as the mean or median. However, RT distributions are typically positively skewed, bounded at zero, and characterized by substantial variability both within and between individuals (Wilcox & Keselman, 2003). Moreover, experimental manipulations may affect not only the central tendency of the distribution but also its variability or upper tail. Consequently, analyses based solely on the mean or median can obscure theoretically meaningful distributional effects, motivating calls to move beyond mean-based analyses of RT data (Balota & Yap, 2011).

One way to move beyond the mean is to model the full shape of the RT distribution. Mathematical models provide a flexible way to do so, allowing researchers to draw inferences about the psychological processes—such as memory, attention, and language—that give rise to observed response times. One model that has become especially popular for this purpose is the ex-Gaussian model (Ratcliff & Murdock, 1976). The ex-Gaussian distribution is formed by the convolution of a Gaussian distribution and an exponential distribution and is characterized by three parameters: \mu, \sigma, and \tau. The parameter \mu represents the mean of the Gaussian component, whereas \sigma represents its standard deviation. The \tau parameter represents both the mean and standard deviation of the exponential component and primarily governs the length of the distribution’s positively skewed upper tail. The overall mean of the ex-Gaussian distribution is \mu + \tau , and the standard deviation (SD) is \sqrt{\sigma^2+\tau^2} . Although these parameters have been used to infer cognitive processes such as automaticity (Balota et al., 2008; Dewitt et al., 2018) and cognitive control/inhibition (Kane & Engle, 2003), these interpretations are not inherent to the parameters and should be made cautiously (Matzke & Wagenmakers, 2009). Nevertheless, the ex-Gaussian model can provide a more complete description of RT data than the arithmetic mean alone, which can be disproportionately influenced by the positively skewed tail of the distribution.

The ex-Gaussian model can be fit within a Bayesian framework relatively easily using the {brms} package in R which provides a high-level interface to the probablitsic programming language Stan (Carpenter et al., 2017). The {brms} package supports a wide range of response distributions, including the ex-Gaussian family, and allows researchers to incorporate complex model structures at both the participant and item levels as well as across different parameters. This accessibility has contributed to the growing use of Bayesian ex-Gaussian models in reaction-time research (see Angele et al., 2023, 2024; Geller et al., 2025; Kinoshita & Liong, 2023; Serrano-Carot et al., 2026). However, an important complication arises from the default parameterization used by the R package {brms}. Although the model is straightforward to estimate, its default specification does not necessarily provide direct access to the parameter-specific comparisons researchers often wish to make.

The central problem is that the parameter labeled \mu in the default {brms} ex-Gaussian model does not correspond to the mean of the Gaussian component in the conventional parameterization. Instead, the quantity modeled as \mu in {brms} represents the mean of the full ex-Gaussian distribution. Under the conventional parameterization, this quantity is equal to \mu_{\mathrm{Gaussian}} + \tau. Consequently, regression effects estimated for the default \mu parameter reflect changes in a composite quantity that combines the Gaussian location and exponential tail components. Such effects therefore cannot be interpreted directly as changes in the Gaussian component or in typical response speed. To recover the conventional Gaussian mean, it is necessary to derive it from the model parameters as \mu_{\mathrm{Gaussian}} = \mu_{\mathrm{brms}} - \tau or to specify an alternative parameterization that models the conventional ex-Gaussian parameters directly.

In this commentary, we demonstrate what the default parameterization of the ex-Gaussian model in {brms} actually provides and then show how to specify the model so that its parameters correspond to the conventional ex-Gaussian parameters used in reaction-time research. The goal is to improve the accuracy and interpretability of inferences drawn from these models. This issue is particularly important because the ex-Gaussian distribution is widely used to distinguish changes in the central portion of the RT distribution from changes in its posietively skewed tail.

Simulation Example

To demonstrate the issue with fitting the default ex-Gaussian model in {brms}, I simulate data using a comparison adapted from one of my own studies (Geller et al., 2025) in R [version 4.6.0; R Core Team (2024)]. In Geller et al. (2025), we examined how perceptual disfluency—manipulated by varying the level of blur applied to letter strings influenced reaction times in a lexical decision task (i.e., is this letter string a word?), and whether these effects were related to how well someone remembers the words in a reconition memory task. In that study, low blur words affected early stages of encoding, reflected in the Gaussian location parameter \mu, but not the later, tail component captured by \tau. For the present illustration, however, I construct a sharper scenario in which \mu and \tau differ in opposite directions so that the two conditions share the same expected reaction time. Using the rexgaussian2() function from the {brms.exgaussian} package, I simulate ex-Gaussian data for clearly presented and low-blur words in which \mu is set to 500 ms and 550 ms, \tau to 100 ms and 50 ms, and \sigma to 60 ms in both conditions (see Table 1).

Simulate the Data

We first draw 100 reaction times per condition (200 in total) from the ex-Gaussian distributions defined by these parameters.

# Fix the random seed so the simulated data are identical on every render.
set.seed(1234)

# Number of simulated trials per condition (200 observations in total).
n_per_group <- 100

# One row per trial, labelled by Blur condition (Clear is the reference level).
d <- data.frame(
  Blur = factor(
    rep(c("Clear", "Low-Blur"), each = n_per_group),
    levels = c("Clear", "Low-Blur")
  )
)

# Row index (1 = Clear, 2 = Low-Blur) used to look up each trial's parameters.
parameter_index <- as.integer(d$Blur)

# Draw reaction times from the ex-Gaussian using each trial's condition values.
d$rt <- brms.exgaussian::rexgaussian2(
  n = nrow(d),
  mu = sim_params$mu[parameter_index],
  sigma = sim_params$sigma[parameter_index],
  tau = sim_params$tau[parameter_index]
)

Figure 1 shows the simulated RT distribution for each condition and how the three ex-Gaussian parameters shape it: \mu locates the Gaussian component, \sigma sets its spread, and \tau stretches the right tail. Because the increase in \mu from Clear to Low-Blur is offset by an equal decrease in \tau, the overall mean E(RT) = \mu + \tau is identical (600 ms) in both conditions.

Default {brms} Parameterization

Using this data we fit the default brms ex-Gaussian model. In this parameterization, the parameter labeled mu represents the expected value of the full ex-Gaussian distribution rather than the location of its Gaussian component.

The exponential component is labeled beta. On the response scale, beta corresponds to the classical ex-Gaussian parameter \tau:

\beta_{\texttt{}} = \tau_{\text{classical}}.

Thus, beta and \tau are not substantively different quantities. Both represent the mean of the exponential component. The difference is primarily one of notation. The important difference between the default {} model and the classical parameterization concerns mu:

\mu_{\texttt{}} = \mu_{\text{classical}} + \tau.

In other words, the default {} parameterization uses mu for the expected value of the complete ex-Gaussian distribution, whereas the classical parameterization uses \mu for the location of the Gaussian component.

Both the expected response and the exponential component are allowed to vary across conditions.

Prior Specification

The priors were selected to reflect the known scale of the simulated data while remaining sufficiently broad to allow the model to recover deviations from the generating values.

Show R code 
priors_default <- c(
  # mu intercept: centered at 600 ms, the expected RT (E(RT)) of the reference
  # condition in this default parameterization.
  prior(
    normal(600, 100),
    class = "Intercept"
  ),
  # Effect of Blur on mu: centered at 0 (no assumed direction).
  prior(
    normal(0, 50),
    class = "b"
  ),
  # beta (exponential mean) intercept, on the log scale: exp(log(100)) = 100 ms.
  prior(
    normal(log(100), 0.5),
    class = "Intercept",
    dpar = "beta"
  ),
  # Effect of Blur on beta (log scale): centered at 0 (a ratio of 1).
  prior(
    normal(0, 0.5),
    class = "b",
    dpar = "beta"
  )
)

The intercept prior for the primary mu submodel is

\mu_{\text{Intercept}} \sim \mathcal{N}(600,100).

We center this prior at 600 ms because, in the default parameterization, mu represents the expected RT. The simulated expected RT for the reference condition is

500 + 100 = 600 \text{ ms}.

The standard deviation of 100 ms makes the prior informative about the plausible scale of RTs without tightly constraining the intercept to equal exactly 600 ms.

The prior on the Blur coefficient is

b_{\text{Blur}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,50).

This prior is centered at zero because, before observing the data, positive and negative condition effects are both plausible. A standard deviation of 50 ms allows substantial differences between conditions while placing less prior mass on implausibly large effects.

The beta parameter is positive and uses a log link. Its intercept is therefore modeled on the log scale:

\log(\beta_{\text{Clear}}) \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\log(100),0.5\right).

The prior is centered at \log(100) because the simulated value of the exponential mean for the reference condition is 100 ms. After applying the inverse-link transformation, the prior is centered approximately around

\beta_{\text{Clear}} = 100 \text{ ms}.

The standard deviation of 0.5 is on the log scale. It therefore represents multiplicative rather than additive uncertainty.

The prior on the effect of Blur on beta is

b_{\beta,\text{Blur}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,0.5).

Because this coefficient is also on the log scale, a value of zero represents no difference between conditions:

\exp(0)=1.

The simulated change from 100 ms to 50 ms corresponds to the log ratio

\log\left(\frac{50}{100}\right) = \log(0.5) \approx -0.693.

The prior \mathcal{N}(0,0.5) places reasonable prior mass near this value while still favoring smaller differences.

The model can then be fit as follows:

Show R code 
fit_brms <- brm(
  bf(
    rt ~ Blur,      # mu submodel: here mu is E(RT), not the Gaussian location
    sigma ~ 1,      # constant Gaussian SD
    beta ~ Blur     # exponential mean (the classical tau), allowed to vary
  ),
  data = d,
  prior = priors_default,
  family = exgaussian(),   # 's built-in ex-Gaussian family
  chains = 2,
  iter = 2000,
  warmup = 500,
  backend = "cmdstanr",
  cores = 4,
  seed = 1234              # reproducible draws
)

Estimating Ex-Gaussian Parameters with {easystats}

The {modelbased} package, which is part of the {easystats} ecosystem, can easily be used to obtain condition-specific posterior estimates from a fitted model. The estimate_means() function returns estimated parameters for each condition, back-transformed, so estimates are presented on time scale, whereas estimate_contrasts() computes differences between conditions.

As shown in Table 1, the present data were generated so that the two conditions have the same expected reaction time but differ in the Gaussian and exponential components of the ex-Gaussian distribution.

Under the classical ex-Gaussian parameterization, the expected response is

E(RT) = \mu + \tau.

Thus, the 50-ms increase in \mu for the Low-Blur condition is offset by a 50-ms decrease in \tau. The expected RT is therefore 600 ms in both conditions.

Estimating the Expected Response (\mu)

Condition-specific estimates of the expected response can be obtained with estimate_means() and setting predict argument to “mu”:

  fit_brms,
  by = "Blur",
  predict = "mu",
  centrality = "mean"

Because the primary mu parameter in the default ex-Gaussian model represents E(RT), these estimates should be close to 600 ms in both conditions.

The corresponding contrast can be obtained using estimate_contrasts():

Show R code 
# Default model: Blur contrast in E(RT) (≈ 0 by design).
overall_difference <- estimate_contrasts(
  fit_brms,
  contrast = "Blur",
  centrality = "mean"
)

The posterior estimate of this contrast should be close to zero because the two conditions were generated with the same expected RT:

(550 + 50) - (500 + 100) = 600 - 600 = 0.

Thus, the default model correctly recovers the absence of a difference in the full distributional mean. However, its primary mu coefficient does not directly reveal the 50-ms difference in the Gaussian-location parameter.

Classical Ex-Gaussian Parameterization

As we have shown above, the default paramterization gives you the expected RT of the entire distribution. We can instead fit the model using the {brms.exgaussian} package, which implements the classical ex-Gaussian parameterization. In this model, \mu represents the location of the Gaussian component and \tau represents the mean of the exponential component.

We first define the custom family and Stan functions from the {brms.exgaussian} package:

Show R code 
# Custom family that reparameterizes brms so mu is the Gaussian location.
exg_stanvars <- exgaussian2_stancode()  # Stan code injected into the model
exg_family <- exgaussian2()             # the custom family definition

Prior Specification

The priors for the classical model are specified as follows:

Show R code 
priors_classical <- c(
  # mu intercept: centered at 500 ms, now the Gaussian location of the
  # reference condition (not E(RT)).
  prior(
    normal(500, 100),
    class = "Intercept"
  ),
  # Effect of Blur on mu (Gaussian location): centered at 0.
  prior(
    normal(0, 50),
    class = "b"
  ),
  # tau (exponential mean) intercept, log scale: exp(log(100)) = 100 ms.
  prior(
    normal(log(100), 0.5),
    class = "Intercept",
    dpar = "tau"
  ),
  # Effect of Blur on tau (log scale): centered at 0.
  prior(
    normal(0, 0.5),
    class = "b",
    dpar = "tau"
  ),
  # sigma (Gaussian SD) intercept, log scale: exp(log(60)) = 60 ms.
  prior(
    normal(log(60), 0.5),
    class = "Intercept",
    dpar = "sigma"
  )
)

These priors follow the same reasoning as those for the default model (described above), with two changes. First, the intercept is now centered at 500 ms rather than 600 ms, because in the classical parameterization mu denotes the Gaussian-location parameter (500 ms for the Clear reference condition) rather than the expected RT:

\mu_{\text{Intercept}} \sim \mathcal{N}(500,100).

Second, because this model estimates sigma explicitly, it receives its own weakly informative prior on the log scale, centered at the simulated Gaussian standard deviation of 60 ms:

\log(\sigma) \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\log(60),0.5\right).

The coefficient for Blur on mu and the exponential-component priors are unchanged from the default model; the latter now simply attach to tau rather than beta, reflecting the difference in labels rather than any difference in the underlying quantity.

The model is then fit using:

Show R code 
fit_brms_new <- brm(
  bf(
    rt ~ Blur,      # mu submodel: here mu IS the Gaussian location
    tau ~ Blur,     # exponential mean (the tail), allowed to vary
    sigma ~ 1       # constant Gaussian SD
  ),
  data = d,
  prior = priors_classical,
  family = exg_family,        # custom classical ex-Gaussian family
  stanvars = exg_stanvars,    # supplies its Stan implementation
  chains = 2,
  iter = 2000,
  warmup = 500,
  backend = "cmdstanr",
  cores = 4,
  seed = 1234
)

This example illustrates the central difference between the two parameterizations. The default {brms} ex-Gaussian model directly estimates the expected response and labels that parameter mu. The exponential component is labeled beta, although it corresponds to the classical \tau parameter. In contrast, the {brms.exgaussian} model uses mu for the Gaussian-location parameter and tau for the exponential mean, consistent with the classical parameterization commonly used in reaction-time research.

Real Example

To demonstrate how these parameterization differences play out in empirical research, we re-analyze Experiment 1 from Angele et al. (2023). In their study, participants completed a masked identity priming lexical decision task across two prime exposure durations (33 ms vs. 50 ms) and two priming conditions (Identical vs. Unrelated). Angele et al. (2023) set out to test whether masked priming influences early encoding processes—reflected in a shift of the Gaussian location parameter \mu—without altering the exponential tail parameter \tau (consistent with Forster’s Forster, 1998 savings hypothesis).

To fit their ex-Gaussian models, Angele et al. (2023) used the built-in exgaussian() family in {brms}. As established above, the primary formula in default {brms} models the overall distributional mean E(RT) = \mu_{\text{Gaussian}} + \tau, despite assigning the label mu to this parameter. In their manuscript, Angele et al. (2023) interpreted regression effects on mu as changes in the location of the Gaussian component \mu.

Here, we apply the classical ex-Gaussian parameterization using {brms.exgaussian}, modeling reaction times with maximal random intercepts and random slopes for participants (source) and items (Target) across both \mu (Gaussian location) and \tau (exponential mean).

Prior specification for the classical model:

Show R code 
priors_classical <- c(
  # Gaussian location mu: Intercept & effects
  prior(normal(500, 100), class = "Intercept"),
  prior(normal(0, 100), class = "b", coef = "Condition1"),
  prior(normal(0, 100), class = "b", coef = "PrimeDuration1"),
  prior(normal(0, 100), class = "b", coef = "Condition1:PrimeDuration1"),
  
  # Exponential tail tau (on log scale): Intercept & effects
  prior(normal(log(100), 0.5), class = "Intercept", dpar = "tau"),
  prior(normal(0, 0.5), class = "b", dpar = "tau"),
  
  # Gaussian SD sigma (on log scale): Intercept
  prior(normal(log(60), 0.5), class = "Intercept", dpar = "sigma")
)

Initial Values Specification

When fitting complex hierarchical ex-Gaussian models with random effects on parameters that use log link functions (such as \tau and \sigma), Stan’s default unconstrained random initialization (in [-2, 2]) can cause severe numerical instability. On the log scale, standard deviations randomly drawn near upper bounds (e.g., e^2 \approx 7.38) can generate extreme individual-level parameter deviations (e.g., e^{10} \approx 22{,}000\text{ ms}), causing MCMC chains to immediately reject initial values (log probability evaluates to log(0)) or fail to start.

To prevent initial value rejection and ensure reliable sampling across all chains, we supply an explicit initialization function (init_classical) that sets reasonable starting values for all model parameters:

Show R code 
# Subset data to determine random effect dimensions
data_fit <- exp1_data_to_include %>% 
  filter(corr == 1 & StimulusType == "Word" & rt > 250 & rt < 1800)

N_source <- length(unique(data_fit$source))
N_target <- length(unique(data_fit$Target))

# Function generating stable initial values for all Stan parameters
init_classical <- function() {
  list(
    Intercept = 500,           # Gaussian location (identity link)
    Intercept_sigma = log(60), # Gaussian SD (log link)
    Intercept_tau = log(100),  # Exponential tail (log link)
    b = rep(0, 3),             # Fixed effects on mu
    b_tau = rep(0, 3),         # Fixed effects on tau
    sd_1 = rep(30, 4),         # Participant SDs for mu (ms)
    sd_2 = rep(30, 4),         # Item SDs for mu (ms)
    sd_3 = rep(0.1, 4),        # Participant SDs for tau (log scale, small to prevent overflow)
    sd_4 = rep(0.1, 4),        # Item SDs for tau (log scale, small to prevent overflow)
    z_1 = matrix(0, nrow = 4, ncol = N_source),  # Participant random effects for mu
    z_2 = matrix(0, nrow = 4, ncol = N_target),  # Item random effects for mu
    z_3 = matrix(0, nrow = 4, ncol = N_source),  # Participant random effects for tau
    z_4 = matrix(0, nrow = 4, ncol = N_target),  # Item random effects for tau
    L_1 = diag(4),             # Cholesky factor for participant random effects (mu)
    L_2 = diag(4),             # Cholesky factor for item random effects (mu)
    L_3 = diag(4),             # Cholesky factor for participant random effects (tau)
    L_4 = diag(4)              # Cholesky factor for item random effects (tau)
  )
}

The classical ex-Gaussian model for Experiment 1 is specified and fit as follows:

Show R code 
# Setup custom classical ex-Gaussian family from {brms.exgaussian}
exg_stanvars <- exgaussian2_stancode()
exg_family <- exgaussian2()

# Fit classical ex-Gaussian hierarchical model to Experiment 1 RT data
blmm_exp1_classical_rt <- brm(
  data = data_fit, 
  formula = bf(
    rt ~ Condition * PrimeDuration + (1 + Condition * PrimeDuration|source) + (1 + Condition * PrimeDuration|Target), 
    tau ~ Condition * PrimeDuration + (1 + Condition * PrimeDuration|source) + (1 + Condition * PrimeDuration|Target),
    sigma ~ 1
  ),
  warmup = 1000,
  iter = 5000,
  chains = 4,
  prior = priors_classical,
  family = exg_family,
  stanvars = exg_stanvars,
  inits = init_classical,
  cores = 4, 
  backend = "cmdstanr", 
  threads = threading(2)
)

Depending on the hardware used, this model may take more than 30 minutes to fit. Because of this, it is recommended to fit it once and then save the fitted model object to a file for later use.

Gaussian-Location Parameter (\mu)

Under the classical parameterization, the primary regression formula directly estimates the Gaussian location parameter \mu. Table 10 displays the estimated posterior means for \mu across priming conditions and exposure durations, and Table 11 presents the model’s population-level predictor effects (main effect of relatedness, main effect of prime duration, and their interaction) alongside simple priming contrasts (Unrelated minus Identical) at each prime duration.

As shown in Table 11, under the classical parameterization, the true Gaussian location \mu shows a robust overall main effect of relatedness (28.5, 95% CrI [24.7, 32.4]). Crucially, the main effect of prime duration on \mu is non-credible (-3.1, 95% CrI [-6.5, 0.2], with the 95% CrI containing zero), in contrast to the location shift reported under the default parameterization. The model also reveals a credible positive interaction between relatedness and prime duration (17.1, 95% CrI [10.5, 23.8]), indicating that the priming effect on \mu was significantly larger at 50 ms (37.1, 95% CrI [31.9, 42.2]) than at 33 ms (20.0, 95% CrI [14.9, 25.0]).

Exponential Component (\tau)

Table 12 displays the estimated exponential mean \tau across conditions, and Table 13 presents the corresponding fixed effects and priming contrasts.

For the exponential component \tau, the main effect of relatedness is small and non-credible (3.0, 95% CrI [-1.9, 8.1]), as is the interaction term (2.1, 95% CrI [-8.0, 12.8]; log scale b = 0.022). Consequently, simple priming differences at 33 ms (2.0, 95% CrI [-5.4, 9.5]) and 50 ms (4.1, 95% CrI [-2.9, 11.2]) remain non-credible, consistent with Forster’s (1998) savings hypothesis. Comparison with Angele et al. (2023)

Comparing these classical ex-Gaussian estimates with those originally reported by Angele et al. (2023) highlights the practical consequences of model parameterization for theoretical inferences (Table 14).

  1. Parameter Interpretation: Angele et al. (2023) reported fixed effects for relatedness, prime duration, and their interaction from default {brms} under the assumption that mu represented the Gaussian location component \mu_{\text{Gaussian}}. In reality, brms default mu estimates E(RT) = \mu_{\text{Gaussian}} + \tau. Consequently, Angele et al. (2023) reported overall distributional mean differences under the label of Gaussian location shifts.

  2. Divergent Conclusions on Prime Duration: A critical theoretical discrepancy emerges regarding the main effect of prime duration. Based on their default {brms} model, Angele et al. (2023) concluded that increasing prime exposure duration from 33 ms to 50 ms produced a credible downward shift in the Gaussian location parameter \mu (b = -10.9\text{ ms}, 95% CrI [-14.5, -7.3]). However, the classical parameterization reveals that the true main effect of prime duration on \mu_{\text{Gaussian}} is non-credible (-3.1\text{ ms}, 95% CrI [-6.5, 0.2], with the 95% CrI containing zero). Instead, the overall reduction in RTs at 50 ms prime duration was driven almost entirely by a compression of the exponential tail \tau (-9.2\text{ ms}, 95% CrI [-14.7, -3.5]; log scale b = -0.085, 95% CrI [-0.137, -0.032]). Because default {brms} models E(RT) = \mu_{\text{Gaussian}} + \tau, the -10.9\text{ ms} effect reported by Angele et al. (2023) as a location shift was actually the composite sum of a non-credible \mu shift (-3.1\text{ ms}) and a credible \tau tail reduction (-9.2\text{ ms}).

  3. Priming Effects and Interaction: For the priming manipulation, the main effect of relatedness on \mu_{\text{Gaussian}} (28.5\text{ ms}, 95% CrI [24.7, 32.4]) and the positive interaction (17.1\text{ ms}, 95% CrI [10.5, 23.8]) closely mirror the E(RT) effects reported by Angele et al. (2023) (main effect \sim 31.5\text{ ms}, interaction \sim 19.3\text{ ms}). The estimates for the two parameterizations align closely for the priming effect because \tau showed no credible priming differences (3.0\text{ ms}, 95% CrI [-1.9, 8.1]). When \Delta \tau \approx 0, changes in the distributional mean \Delta E(RT) track changes in the Gaussian location \Delta \mu nearly 1-to-1.

  4. Methodological Takeaway: This empirical comparison demonstrates that misinterpreting default {brms} parameters can lead to subtle misattributions regarding which distributional component is altered by an experimental manipulation. In Angele et al. (2023), a tail-shortening effect of prime duration (\Delta \tau = -9.2\text{ ms}) was attributed to the Gaussian location parameter (mu). Adopting the classical parameterization resolves this misattribution and ensures that theoretical claims align with the underlying distributional parameters.

Conclusion

With this commentary, I hope to encourage researchers to use the appropriate ex-Gaussian parameterization when modeling reaction-time data with the {brms} package. The ex-Gaussian distribution provides a powerful framework for moving beyond analyses of the mean by separating differences in the central portion of the reaction-time distribution from differences in its right tail.

However, this flexibility also requires careful attention to model parameterization. In particular, researchers should not assume that parameters with familiar labels necessarily have the same interpretation across software implementations. Researchers should therefore examine the statistical definition and underlying implementation of a model before fitting it and interpreting its parameters. This is especially important when using flexible modeling software that may adopt parameterizations that differ from those commonly used in a particular research literature. I hope this commentary serves both as a practical guide to fitting the classical ex-Gaussian model in {brms} and as a broader reminder that convenient software defaults should not replace careful consideration of what a model actually estimates.

Over the past two decades, psychology has seen a substantial increase in the application of Bayesian data analysis methods as a supplement to widely used frequentist methods [@pfadt2025methodological]. This shift has been driven by the increasing availability of computational tools that have made fitting Bayesian models both practical and efficient. Programming languages like R [@R], Python [@python], and Julia [@julia] provide libraries and packages that allow researchers to fit Bayesian models with little technical overhead. In addition, growing dissatisfaction with null-hypothesis significance testing (NHST) and its associated pitfalls (e.g., p-value misinterpretation and dichotomous thinking), together with the replication crisis/credibility revolution [@vazire2018], has pushed many researchers toward statistical alternatives.

One domain that has seen an increase in the adoption of Bayesian methods is the analysis of response time (RT) data in cognitive sciences where RTs are routinely used as measures of processing speed, decision difficulty, and cognitive efficiency. Traditionally, RT data have often been analyzed using summary statistics such as the mean or median. However, RT distributions are typically positively skewed, bounded at zero, and characterized by substantial variability both within and between individuals [@wilcox]. Moreover, experimental manipulations may affect not only the central tendency of the distribution but also its variability or upper tail. Consequently, analyses based solely on the mean or median can obscure theoretically meaningful distributional effects, motivating calls to move beyond mean-based analyses of RT data [@balota2011].

One way to move beyond the mean is to model the full shape of the RT distribution. Mathematical models provide a flexible way to do so, allowing researchers to draw inferences about the psychological processes—such as memory, attention, and language—that give rise to observed response times. One model that has become especially popular for this purpose is the ex-Gaussian model [@ratcliff1976]. The ex-Gaussian distribution is formed by the convolution of a Gaussian distribution and an exponential distribution and is characterized by three parameters: $\mu$, $\sigma$, and $\tau$. The parameter $\mu$ represents the mean of the Gaussian component, whereas $\sigma$ represents its standard deviation. The $\tau$ parameter represents both the mean and standard deviation of the exponential component and primarily governs the length of the distribution’s positively skewed upper tail. The overall mean of the ex-Gaussian distribution is $\mu$ + $\tau$ , and the standard deviation (SD) is $\sqrt{\sigma^2+\tau^2}$ . Although these parameters have been used to infer cognitive processes such as automaticity [@dewitt2018; @balota2008] and cognitive control/inhibition [@kane2003], these interpretations are not inherent to the parameters and should be made cautiously [@matzke2009]. Nevertheless, the ex-Gaussian model can provide a more complete description of RT data than the arithmetic mean alone, which can be disproportionately influenced by the positively skewed tail of the distribution.

The ex-Gaussian model can be fit within a Bayesian framework relatively easily using the {brms} package in R which provides a high-level interface to the probablitsic programming language Stan [@stan]. The {brms} package supports a wide range of response distributions, including the ex-Gaussian family, and allows researchers to incorporate complex model structures at both the participant and item levels as well as across different parameters. This accessibility has contributed to the growing use of Bayesian ex-Gaussian models in reaction-time research [see @angele2023; @angele2024reading; @geller2025; @kinoshita2023; @serranocarot2026]. However, an important complication arises from the default parameterization used by the R package {brms}. Although the model is straightforward to estimate, its default specification does not necessarily provide direct access to the parameter-specific comparisons researchers often wish to make.

The central problem is that the parameter labeled $\mu$ in the default {brms} ex-Gaussian model does not correspond to the mean of the Gaussian component in the conventional parameterization. Instead, the quantity modeled as $\mu$ in {brms} represents the mean of the full ex-Gaussian distribution. Under the conventional parameterization, this quantity is equal to $\mu_{\mathrm{Gaussian}} + \tau$. Consequently, regression effects estimated for the default $\mu$ parameter reflect changes in a composite quantity that combines the Gaussian location and exponential tail components. Such effects therefore cannot be interpreted directly as changes in the Gaussian component or in typical response speed. To recover the conventional Gaussian mean, it is necessary to derive it from the model parameters as $\mu_{\mathrm{Gaussian}} =
\mu_{\mathrm{brms}} - \tau$ or to specify an alternative parameterization that models the conventional ex-Gaussian parameters directly.

In this commentary, we demonstrate what the default parameterization of the ex-Gaussian model in {brms} actually provides and then show how to specify the model so that its parameters correspond to the conventional ex-Gaussian parameters used in reaction-time research. The goal is to improve the accuracy and interpretability of inferences drawn from these models. This issue is particularly important because the ex-Gaussian distribution is widely used to distinguish changes in the central portion of the RT distribution from changes in its posietively skewed tail.

## Simulation Example

To demonstrate the issue with fitting the default ex-Gaussian model in {brms}, I simulate data using a comparison adapted from one of my own studies [@geller2025] in R [version 4.6.0; @R]. In @geller2025, we examined how perceptual disfluency—manipulated by varying the level of blur applied to letter strings influenced reaction times in a lexical decision task (i.e., is this letter string a word?), and whether these effects were related to how well someone remembers the words in a reconition memory task. In that study, low blur words affected early stages of encoding, reflected in the Gaussian location parameter $\mu$, but not the later, tail component captured by $\tau$. clean_estimate <- function(x, digits = 1) {
```{r}
  d <- as.data.frame(x)

  # combine Level1/Level2 (contrast tables) into a single "Contrast" label
  l1 <- names(d)[tolower(names(d)) == "level1"]

  if (length(l1) && length(l2)) {
    d[["Contrast"]] <- paste(d[[l1]], "-", d[[l2]])
    d[[l1]] <- NULL
    d[[l2]] <- NULL
  }
  if (all(c("CI_low", "CI_high") %in% names(d))) {
    d[["95% CrI"]] <- paste0(
      "[", round(d$CI_low, digits), ", ", round(d$CI_high, digits), "]"
    )
    d$CI_low <- NULL
    d$CI_high <- NULL
  }
  num <- setdiff(names(d)[vapply(d, is.numeric, logical(1))], "pd")
  d[num] <- lapply(d[num], round, digits)
  if ("pd" %in% names(d)) d$pd <- round(d$pd, 3)
  # order: Contrast first, estimate next, then CrI and pd last
  lead <- intersect("Contrast", names(d))
  tail_cols <- intersect(c("95% CrI", "pd"), names(d))
  d[, c(lead, setdiff(names(d), c(lead, tail_cols)), tail_cols), drop = FALSE]
}
```

## Default {brms} Parameterization

Using this data we fit the default `brms` ex-Gaussian model. In this parameterization, the parameter labeled `mu` represents the expected value of the full ex-Gaussian distribution rather than the location of its Gaussian component.

The exponential component is labeled `beta`. On the response scale, `beta` corresponds to the classical ex-Gaussian parameter $\tau$:

$$\beta_{\texttt{}} = \tau_{\text{classical}}.$$

Thus, `beta` and $\tau$ are not substantively different quantities. Both represent the mean of the exponential component. The difference is primarily one of notation. The important difference between the default {} model and the classical parameterization concerns `mu`:

$$\mu_{\texttt{}} = \mu_{\text{classical}} + \tau.$$

In other words, the default **{`}`** parameterization uses `mu` for the expected value of the complete ex-Gaussian distribution, whereas the classical parameterization uses $\mu$ for the location of the Gaussian component.

Both the expected response and the exponential component are allowed to vary across conditions.

### Prior Specification

The priors were selected to reflect the known scale of the simulated data while remaining sufficiently broad to allow the model to recover deviations from the generating values.

```{r}
#| label: priors-default
priors_default <- c(
  # mu intercept: centered at 600 ms, the expected RT (E(RT)) of the reference
  # condition in this default parameterization.
  prior(
    normal(600, 100),
    class = "Intercept"
  ),
  # Effect of Blur on mu: centered at 0 (no assumed direction).
  prior(
    normal(0, 50),
    class = "b"
  ),
  # beta (exponential mean) intercept, on the log scale: exp(log(100)) = 100 ms.
  prior(
    normal(log(100), 0.5),
    class = "Intercept",
    dpar = "beta"
  ),
  # Effect of Blur on beta (log scale): centered at 0 (a ratio of 1).
  prior(
    normal(0, 0.5),
    class = "b",
    dpar = "beta"
  )
)
```

The intercept prior for the primary `mu` submodel is

$$\mu_{\text{Intercept}}
\sim
\mathcal{N}(600,100).$$

We center this prior at 600 ms because, in the default parameterization, `mu` represents the expected RT. The simulated expected RT for the reference condition is

$$500 + 100 = 600 \text{ ms}.$$

The standard deviation of 100 ms makes the prior informative about the plausible scale of RTs without tightly constraining the intercept to equal exactly 600 ms.

The prior on the `Blur` coefficient is

$$b_{\text{Blur}}
\sim
\mathcal{N}(0,50).$$

This prior is centered at zero because, before observing the data, positive and negative condition effects are both plausible. A standard deviation of 50 ms allows substantial differences between conditions while placing less prior mass on implausibly large effects.

The `beta` parameter is positive and uses a log link. Its intercept is therefore modeled on the log scale:

$$\log(\beta_{\text{Clear}})
\sim
\mathcal{N}\left(\log(100),0.5\right).$$

The prior is centered at $\log(100)$ because the simulated value of the exponential mean for the reference condition is 100 ms. After applying the inverse-link transformation, the prior is centered approximately around

$$\beta_{\text{Clear}} = 100 \text{ ms}.$$

The standard deviation of 0.5 is on the log scale. It therefore represents multiplicative rather than additive uncertainty.

The prior on the effect of `Blur` on `beta` is

$$b_{\beta,\text{Blur}}
\sim
\mathcal{N}(0,0.5).$$

Because this coefficient is also on the log scale, a value of zero represents no difference between conditions:

$$\exp(0)=1.$$

The simulated change from 100 ms to 50 ms corresponds to the log ratio

$$\log\left(\frac{50}{100}\right) = \log(0.5) \approx -0.693.$$

The prior $\mathcal{N}(0,0.5)$ places reasonable prior mass near this value while still favoring smaller differences.

The model can then be fit as follows:

```{r}
#| label: fit--default
fit_brms <- brm(
  bf(
    rt ~ Blur,      # mu submodel: here mu is E(RT), not the Gaussian location
    sigma ~ 1,      # constant Gaussian SD
    beta ~ Blur     # exponential mean (the classical tau), allowed to vary
  ),
  data = d,
  prior = priors_default,
  family = exgaussian(),   # 's built-in ex-Gaussian family
  chains = 2,
  iter = 2000,
  warmup = 500,
  backend = "cmdstanr",
  cores = 4,
  seed = 1234              # reproducible draws
)
```

## Classical Ex-Gaussian Parameterization

As we have shown above, the default paramterization gives you the expected RT of the entire distribution. We can instead fit the model using the `{brms.exgaussian}` package, which implements the classical ex-Gaussian parameterization. In this model, $\mu$ represents the location of the Gaussian component and $\tau$ represents the mean of the exponential component.

We first define the custom family and Stan functions from the {brms.exgaussian} package:

```{r}
#| label: exgaussian-family-setup
# Custom family that reparameterizes brms so mu is the Gaussian location.
exg_stanvars <- exgaussian2_stancode()  # Stan code injected into the model
exg_family <- exgaussian2()             # the custom family definition
```

### Prior Specification

The priors for the classical model are specified as follows:

```{r}
#| label: priors-classical
priors_classical <- c(
  # mu intercept: centered at 500 ms, now the Gaussian location of the
  # reference condition (not E(RT)).
  prior(
    normal(500, 100),
    class = "Intercept"
  ),
  # Effect of Blur on mu (Gaussian location): centered at 0.
  prior(
    normal(0, 50),
    class = "b"
  ),
  # tau (exponential mean) intercept, log scale: exp(log(100)) = 100 ms.
  prior(
    normal(log(100), 0.5),
    class = "Intercept",
    dpar = "tau"
  ),
  # Effect of Blur on tau (log scale): centered at 0.
  prior(
    normal(0, 0.5),
    class = "b",
    dpar = "tau"
  ),
  # sigma (Gaussian SD) intercept, log scale: exp(log(60)) = 60 ms.
  prior(
    normal(log(60), 0.5),
    class = "Intercept",
    dpar = "sigma"
  )
)
```

These priors follow the same reasoning as those for the default model (described above), with two changes. First, the intercept is now centered at 500 ms rather than 600 ms, because in the classical parameterization `mu` denotes the Gaussian-location parameter (500 ms for the Clear reference condition) rather than the expected RT:

$$\mu_{\text{Intercept}} \sim \mathcal{N}(500,100).$$

Second, because this model estimates `sigma` explicitly, it receives its own weakly informative prior on the log scale, centered at the simulated Gaussian standard deviation of 60 ms:

$$\log(\sigma) \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\log(60),0.5\right).$$

The coefficient for `Blur` on `mu` and the exponential-component priors are unchanged from the default model; the latter now simply attach to `tau` rather than `beta`, reflecting the difference in labels rather than any difference in the underlying quantity.

The model is then fit using:

```{r}
#| label: fit-brms-classical
fit_brms_new <- brm(
  bf(
    rt ~ Blur,      # mu submodel: here mu IS the Gaussian location
    tau ~ Blur,     # exponential mean (the tail), allowed to vary
    sigma ~ 1       # constant Gaussian SD
  ),
  data = d,
  prior = priors_classical,
  family = exg_family,        # custom classical ex-Gaussian family
  stanvars = exg_stanvars,    # supplies its Stan implementation
  chains = 2,
  iter = 2000,
  warmup = 500,
  backend = "cmdstanr",
  cores = 4,
  seed = 1234
)
```

## Real Example

To demonstrate how these parameterization differences play out in empirical research, we re-analyze Experiment 1 from @angele2023. In their study, participants completed a masked identity priming lexical decision task across two prime exposure durations (33 ms vs. 50 ms) and two priming conditions (Identical vs. Unrelated). @angele2023 set out to test whether masked priming influences early encoding processes—reflected in a shift of the Gaussian location parameter $\mu$—without altering the exponential tail parameter $\tau$ [consistent with Forster's @forster1998pros savings hypothesis].

To fit their ex-Gaussian models, @angele2023 used the built-in `exgaussian()` family in {brms}. As established above, the primary formula in default {brms} models the overall distributional mean $E(RT) = \mu_{\text{Gaussian}} + \tau$, despite assigning the label `mu` to this parameter. In their manuscript, @angele2023 interpreted regression effects on `mu` as changes in the location of the Gaussian component $\mu$.

Here, we apply the classical ex-Gaussian parameterization using `{brms.exgaussian}`, modeling reaction times with maximal random intercepts and random slopes for participants (`source`) and items (`Target`) across both $\mu$ (Gaussian location) and $\tau$ (exponential mean).

Prior specification for the classical model:


### Initial Values Specification

When fitting complex hierarchical ex-Gaussian models with random effects on parameters that use log link functions (such as $\tau$ and $\sigma$), Stan's default unconstrained random initialization (in $[-2, 2]$) can cause severe numerical instability. On the log scale, standard deviations randomly drawn near upper bounds (e.g., $e^2 \approx 7.38$) can generate extreme individual-level parameter deviations (e.g., $e^{10} \approx 22{,}000\text{ ms}$), causing MCMC chains to immediately reject initial values (`log probability evaluates to log(0)`) or fail to start.

To prevent initial value rejection and ensure reliable sampling across all chains, we supply an explicit initialization function (`init_classical`) that sets reasonable starting values for all model parameters:

```{r}
#| label: inits-real-exp1
#| eval: false

# Subset data to determine random effect dimensions
data_fit <- exp1_data_to_include %>% 
  filter(corr == 1 & StimulusType == "Word" & rt > 250 & rt < 1800)

N_source <- length(unique(data_fit$source))
N_target <- length(unique(data_fit$Target))

# Function generating stable initial values for all Stan parameters
init_classical <- function() {
  list(
    Intercept = 500,           # Gaussian location (identity link)
    Intercept_sigma = log(60), # Gaussian SD (log link)
    Intercept_tau = log(100),  # Exponential tail (log link)
    b = rep(0, 3),             # Fixed effects on mu
    b_tau = rep(0, 3),         # Fixed effects on tau
    sd_1 = rep(30, 4),         # Participant SDs for mu (ms)
    sd_2 = rep(30, 4),         # Item SDs for mu (ms)
    sd_3 = rep(0.1, 4),        # Participant SDs for tau (log scale, small to prevent overflow)
    sd_4 = rep(0.1, 4),        # Item SDs for tau (log scale, small to prevent overflow)
    z_1 = matrix(0, nrow = 4, ncol = N_source),  # Participant random effects for mu
    z_2 = matrix(0, nrow = 4, ncol = N_target),  # Item random effects for mu
    z_3 = matrix(0, nrow = 4, ncol = N_source),  # Participant random effects for tau
    z_4 = matrix(0, nrow = 4, ncol = N_target),  # Item random effects for tau
    L_1 = diag(4),             # Cholesky factor for participant random effects (mu)
    L_2 = diag(4),             # Cholesky factor for item random effects (mu)
    L_3 = diag(4),             # Cholesky factor for participant random effects (tau)
    L_4 = diag(4)              # Cholesky factor for item random effects (tau)
  )
}
```

### Comparison with Angele et al. (2023)

Comparing these classical ex-Gaussian estimates with those originally reported by @angele2023 highlights the practical consequences of model parameterization for theoretical inferences (@tbl-real-comparison).

1. **Parameter Interpretation**: @angele2023 reported fixed effects for relatedness, prime duration, and their interaction from default {brms} under the assumption that `mu` represented the Gaussian location component $\mu_{\text{Gaussian}}$. In reality, `brms` default `mu` estimates $E(RT) = \mu_{\text{Gaussian}} + \tau$. Consequently, @angele2023 reported overall distributional mean differences under the label of Gaussian location shifts.

2. **Divergent Conclusions on Prime Duration**: A critical theoretical discrepancy emerges regarding the main effect of prime duration. Based on their default {brms} model, @angele2023 concluded that increasing prime exposure duration from 33 ms to 50 ms produced a credible downward shift in the Gaussian location parameter $\mu$ ($b = -10.9\text{ ms}$, 95% CrI [$-14.5, -7.3$]). However, the classical parameterization reveals that the true main effect of prime duration on $\mu_{\text{Gaussian}}$ is **non-credible** ($-3.1\text{ ms}$, 95% CrI [$-6.5, 0.2$], with the 95% CrI containing zero). Instead, the overall reduction in RTs at 50 ms prime duration was driven almost entirely by a compression of the exponential tail $\tau$ ($-9.2\text{ ms}$, 95% CrI [$-14.7, -3.5$]; log scale $b = -0.085$, 95% CrI [$-0.137, -0.032$]). Because default {brms} models $E(RT) = \mu_{\text{Gaussian}} + \tau$, the $-10.9\text{ ms}$ effect reported by @angele2023 as a location shift was actually the composite sum of a non-credible $\mu$ shift ($-3.1\text{ ms}$) and a credible $\tau$ tail reduction ($-9.2\text{ ms}$).

3. **Priming Effects and Interaction**: For the priming manipulation, the main effect of relatedness on $\mu_{\text{Gaussian}}$ ($28.5\text{ ms}$, 95% CrI [$24.7, 32.4$]) and the positive interaction ($17.1\text{ ms}$, 95% CrI [$10.5, 23.8$]) closely mirror the $E(RT)$ effects reported by @angele2023 (main effect $\sim 31.5\text{ ms}$, interaction $\sim 19.3\text{ ms}$). The estimates for the two parameterizations align closely for the priming effect because $\tau$ showed no credible priming differences ($3.0\text{ ms}$, 95% CrI [$-1.9, 8.1$]). When $\Delta \tau \approx 0$, changes in the distributional mean $\Delta E(RT)$ track changes in the Gaussian location $\Delta \mu$ nearly 1-to-1.

4. **Methodological Takeaway**: This empirical comparison demonstrates that misinterpreting default {brms} parameters can lead to subtle misattributions regarding *which* distributional component is altered by an experimental manipulation. In @angele2023, a tail-shortening effect of prime duration ($\Delta \tau = -9.2\text{ ms}$) was attributed to the Gaussian location parameter (`mu`). Adopting the classical parameterization resolves this misattribution and ensures that theoretical claims align with the underlying distributional parameters.

## Conclusion

With this commentary, I hope to encourage researchers to use the appropriate ex-Gaussian parameterization when modeling reaction-time data with the {brms} package. The ex-Gaussian distribution provides a powerful framework for moving beyond analyses of the mean by separating differences in the central portion of the reaction-time distribution from differences in its right tail.

However, this flexibility also requires careful attention to model parameterization. In particular, researchers should not assume that parameters with familiar labels necessarily have the same interpretation across software implementations. Researchers should therefore examine the statistical definition and underlying implementation of a model before fitting it and interpreting its parameters. This is especially important when using flexible modeling software that may adopt parameterizations that differ from those commonly used in a particular research literature. I hope this commentary serves both as a practical guide to fitting the classical ex-Gaussian model in {brms} and as a broader reminder that convenient software defaults should not replace careful consideration of what a model actually estimates.
